Gumbo Gooder Than Grits

This is a simple but delicious gumbo to give you the basics. The technique to make the roux is a little intimidating at first but is very quick and easy. If you burn it, just start over. It's only flour and oil so relax. Just make sure to have all Ingredients prepped and available once you start. The mixture of celery, bell pepper and onion is call the Trinity in cajun and creole cooking. It is similar to mirepoix (carrot, onion, celery) in French cuisine.

Ingredients

  • 1/3 cup oil (canola or any other high temp oil with a neutral flavor)
  • a bit more oil to sear chicken
  • 1/3 cup flour
  • 1 bell pepper
  • 1-2 ribs of celery
  • 1 onion
  • 1 jalapeno (optional)
  • 5 or 6 cloves of garlic
  • 12oz beer (anything but lite beer. I use a scotch ale)
  • 2 quarts chicken stock
  • 2 lbs andouille sausage (any smoked sausage will work)
  • 1-2 lbs boneless, skinless chicken thighs
  • 3-5 bay leaves
  • cayenne pepper to taste
  • 2 tbsp smoked paprika
  • black pepper
  • salt

Steps

  1. Dice your onion, celery, bell pepper and jalapeno if you like a spicier gumbo. They don't need to be perfect but keep them pretty close in size. You can put them all together in one bowl or keep them separate. Mince your garlic and keep it separate.
  2. Cut the sausage in to bite size pieces. Either round pieces or chunks will work.
  3. To make your roux, heat a large stock pot on medium-high. Add your oil. When the oil starts heating up, add the flour. Whisk constantly until roux turns dark brown, close to a dark chocolate color. Do not walk away during this step as the roux can easily burn in a few seconds. If it burns, just start over. Everyone burns a roux at some point.
  4. When roux is done, add celery, carrot, onion (your Trinity), and bay leaves to the pot. Adding to the hot roux will start to cook the trinity very quickly so stir frequently for the first minute or so. When the mixture has calmed down a bit and the trinity starts to become a bit translucent, add the minced garlic. Cook for about one more minute.
  5. Add your beer to the pot. Cook for a few minutes until it reduces a bit and the alcohol smell has cooked off.
  6. Add in your chicken stock, sausage, smoked paprika, cayenne, and black pepper. Cayenne is to taste but this needs lots of black pepper. Basically use your pepper mill and have at it until your arm gets tired. You can add some salt at this point but don't go overboard. You will want to adjust for it later after this cooks down.
  7. The chicken thighs can be seared off at any point. If you are good with multi-tasking, you can do it while the trinity cooks but it's easier to do after adding your stock. To sear, heat a large pan to medium or medium-high. Add just enough oil to coat pan. Season both side of chicken thighs with salt and pepper. Sear on both sides. This can be done in batches if your pan is not big enough to hold all the chicken without over crowding the pan. Add the chicken to the stock pot. There should be some crispy bits in the bottom of the pan. Add a bit of the gumbo liquid to the pan and scrape those bit up. Pour that back in the pot.
  8. Bring pot to a soft boil and reduce heat to low. Cover pot and simmer for 4-6 hours, stirring occasionally. The longer the better. There will be a fair bit of oil on top that can be skimmed if you want. I don't skim much or any because it tastes amazing. At this point you will taste for salt. There's a fair bit of salt in the sausage so it may be fine but add more if you think it needs more.
  9. Serve in a bowl with a scoop of white rice or just by itself if you don't feel like making rice.
